The Beginning Adopting a Stray Cat

How it all started

I was once a teacher with Indonesia Mengajar, a program that sends young people to teach in remote villages in Indonesia for a year. The program was featured in The New York Times a few years back.

I was assigned to teach in Rote Island, of East Nusa Tenggara province with 8 other young people, each living and teaching in a different village. The village I lived in was only 30 minutes away from the town, but the island had little public transportation. Initially, I often relied on others to take me to places on a motorbike. Then I decided that it was imperative for me to be able to ride a motorbike. And that's how it began.

Adoption process

This is how I got my very first motorbike:

  1. Learn how to start a motorbike
  2. Test drive a friend's motorbike
  3. Ask a friend's friend to buy a used motorbike from the main island
  4. Have the motorbike transported on a ship to the village
  5. Go to town to apply for a driving license
  6. Ride the motorbike! Yippee!

Except that this second-hand motorbike had some issues.

When I told my friends that I was buying a motorbike, they were surprised. They were more surprised to know that I blindly trusted someone I didn't know to buy me the bike from another island. In Indonesia we had the expression, "Buying a cat in a bag", for things like this - which means that we don't know what we're buying.

It turned out that it was a stray cat, or what we called "kucing garong".

Though troublesome as it was when I first got it, it brought me to a lot of adventures - up and down a mountain, across the river, and on rocky roads. The stray cat proved to be a trusted and faithful companion.

My teachers

  • My pastor

    • He took me one of the most difficult places to reach: the church. Our church was located in a village down the mountain and the road that went there was rocky and had sharp turns. I had my first motorcycle accident when going to the church. But I learned that if I could do such difficult road, I could do many other kinds of road.

  • My friend

    • He was the first to lend me his motorbike for me to 'abuse'. I wouldn't dare practicing with someone else's motorbike in fear of ruining it.

  • My fellow female teacher

    • She was my colleague at school, and one of my closest local friends in the island. She was also one of the coolest female riders. I learned from her that women can ride on the most difficult roads too.

  • My students

    • My students took pride in claiming that they taught me how to ride a motorbike. Some of the young boys really did know how to ride them. When I first got my motorbike, they would run besides me and literally instructing me when to switch gear and break.
